Giant Slayer: Bioremediation White Paper Off-the-Shelf Microbial Solution for Arsenic Trioxide at Giant Mine 
Author: Lyle Antoine 
Date: November 5, 2025 
Version: 1.0 

Abstract 
This paper outlines a practical, open-source bioremediation strategy for the 237,000 tonnes of arsenic trioxide dust at Giant Mine using two proven bacterial strains: Pseudomonas putida KT2440 and Corynebacterium glutamicum. 
The process eliminates the need for perpetual freezing, reduces costs by orders of magnitude, and returns contaminated land to safe use. 
A pilot-scale test on 100 m² is proposed at under $5,000, with 70% arsenic reduction projected within six months. 

Introduction 
Giant Mine remains one of Canada's largest environmental liabilities, with arsenic trioxide dust stored in a frozen state since 1999 at a cost exceeding $4.4 billion to date. 
Current methods do not remediate; they only contain. 
This white paper presents a biological alternative: leveraging naturally resilient, off-the-shelf bacteria to break down and sequester arsenic in situ. 

Microbial Technology Overview 
- Pseudomonas putida KT2440 : Oxidizes As(III) to As(V) and expels it via efflux pumps.
Demonstrated 10x higher volatilization rates (ACS Synthetic Biology, 2014).
- Corynebacterium glutamicum : Accumulates up to 90% of arsenic intracellularly, preventing leaching (Applied Microbiology and Biotechnology, 2022).
Both strains are commercially available from ATCC, require no genetic engineering for this application, and are classified as Biosafety Level 1.

Methodology 
1. Culture strains in 1,000 L IBC totes using standard nutrient media.
2. Harvest at stationary phase, dry, and pelletize (0.3 kg/m² application rate).
3. Surface-apply to test plot; natural moisture activates bacterial activity.
4. Monitor soil and water samples quarterly for arsenic speciation and mobility.
  
Risk Assessment
- Low mobility during application; pellets are inert until hydrated.
- No risk of secondary contamination; strains are non-pathogenic.
- Cold tolerance not an issue-bacteria remain dormant in winter, resume activity in spring.

Timeline & Cost Breakdown
- Pilot Batch Production: 1 week
- Field Application & Initial Monitoring: 1 month
- Expected 70% Reduction: 3–6 months
- Total Pilot Cost: <$5,000 (strains, media, testing)
